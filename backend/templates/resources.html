Spotting phishing without a tool

Urgency is the single biggest tell

Phrases like "act now," "your account will be suspended," or "verify within 24 hours" exist to short-circuit careful reading. Legitimate organisations rarely threaten immediate account closure over email — when you see manufactured urgency, slow down instead of clicking.

Check where a link actually goes, not what it says

A link's visible text and its actual destination can be completely different. Hover over a link (or long-press on mobile) before clicking, and watch for shortened URLs, raw IP addresses instead of domain names, or a domain that's almost — but not exactly — the brand it claims to be (a swapped letter, an extra word, a different top-level domain).

Sender name vs. sender domain

An email can display any name it wants — "PayPal Security" is just text, not proof of anything. What matters is the actual domain after the @ sign. If the display name references a well-known brand but the domain doesn't match that brand at all, that mismatch alone is a strong signal.

Generic greetings are a small but real signal

"Dear Customer" or "Dear Valued Member" instead of your actual name suggests a mass send rather than a message a real account manager wrote. It's not conclusive on its own, but combined with urgency language or a suspicious link, it adds up.

When in doubt, verify out-of-band

If an email claims your bank or employer needs urgent action, don't use any link or phone number in that email — go directly to the organisation's known website or a phone number you already have on file, and confirm independently.
